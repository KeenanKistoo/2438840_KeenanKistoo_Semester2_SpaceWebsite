Blog Post #01 - Close Reading: Overview of Data Visualisation

This chapter delves into the depth of "The Concept of Data Visualization" (Li, 2020: 17). It provides several different definitions, or rather, understandings of what data visualization can be perceived as. Additionally, this chapter also discusses the different forms that we can consider as a visualization of data.

Each definition differed slightly; however, the central idea shared among many was that data visualization is a method of presenting data to individuals more efficiently, making it easier to comprehend. When looking into this concept further, it is split into two aspects: “information visualization and scientific visualization” (Li, 2020: 19). However, regardless of the division, the data provided needs to be illustrated in an effective way to allow individuals to understand it. My understanding of this is that even though the language used in data sets, spreadsheets, bank statements, and so on, may or may not be complicated, the substantial number of values may be difficult to understand in their “raw” form (Li, 2020: 19).

“Trees” allow data to be displayed to depict importance, structure, time stamps, evolution/growth, or any related instance (Li, 2020: 24). For example, in Figure 1, there is a clear “hierarchical” (Li, 2020: 24) flow to the “skill tree” (FIFA 23, 2022). The locked icon of the tree that is marked A cannot be unlocked unless a series of other branches is unlocked first. The structure is evident and allows the user, in this instance, the player, to view their progression accordingly. This form of data visualisation is not a conventional example like a food chain or a family tree, but it shows progression and importance, which is a fundamental concept of this data visualisation form.

“Maps” are scaled illustrations of a particular location (in a geographical context) or a representation of an aspect, concept, program, or object (Li, 2020: 26). This is a concept that should be easy to understand theoretically since we are exposed to so many, but still, I struggled to grasp it due to the idea that anything can be mapped out. For example, “DNA mapping” and Google Maps are fundamentally different however, both can be classified as a form of a map (Li, 2020: 26). The difference is that a DNA map uses a microscopic scale (this increases the magnification) whereas a map of Johannesburg needs to be scaled down to cover the entire space.

“Tables” allow for information of distinct types to be displayed in one space (Li, 2020: 30). I do think that there is a common misconception that using tables as a data visualisation form refers to an excessive number of values that are all placed within rows and columns. Tables can display text, numbers, check boxes, images, and any other form of data that needs to be compared between various categories. For example, in Figure 2, we see the classes (all the text under headings, which one may interpret as the data types that are being compared) and their corresponding numerical values, whereas, in Figure 3, the numerical values are used to distinguish the data types that are being compared and text is the data that is being displayed.

“Graphs” allow multiple numerical values of a specific or multiple category/s to be displayed visually using lines, bars, and so on (Li, 2020: 34). To some extent, I do think that graphs can be altered or “inaccurate” as the individual/s generating the graph can alter the scale or axis to suit the strategy that they are trying to achieve (Li, 2020: 35); even though the values may be correct, the representation of these values may create a false sense of understanding by the viewers. It is important to represent information honestly and ethically.

There are attributes inherent in “traditional data visualisations” (Li, 2020: 41). The focus of the data visualisation should be on what is being displayed and not on how it is being said. The viewer should easily differentiate between different values, headings, colours, and so on. Yes, graphs, scatter plots, and even maps can be challenging to comprehend, however, once the viewer develops a firm grasp of the data visualisation form, they should easily read the data. The data visualisation should rely on recall and relevancy. It should also act as an aid to understanding the data sets.

Conclusion

During my first attempt at understanding what data visualisation is, I found myself confusing the use of art to represent or explain a concept with data visualisation. After completing this reading, I do think I am in a better position to move forward with my website. When planning this blog post, I set out to write fifty words and an additional example of each data visualisation form. After actually engaging with the reading, various templates, and websites, I found myself lost in endless possibilities. I have proceeded to select a few data visualisation forms that I am most likely to use during the development of my website.
