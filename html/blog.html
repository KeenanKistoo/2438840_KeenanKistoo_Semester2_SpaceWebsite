Blog Post #01 - Close Reading: Overview of Data Visualisation

This chapter delves into the depth of "The Concept of Data Visualization" (Li, 2020: 17). It provides several different definitions, or rather, understandings of what data visualization can be perceived as. Additionally, this chapter also discusses the different forms that we can consider as a visualization of data.

Each definition differed slightly; however, the central idea shared among many was that data visualization is a method of presenting data to individuals more efficiently, making it easier to comprehend. When looking into this concept further, it is split into two aspects: “information visualization and scientific visualization” (Li, 2020: 19). However, regardless of the division, the data provided needs to be illustrated in an effective way to allow individuals to understand it. My understanding of this is that even though the language used in data sets, spreadsheets, bank statements, and so on, may or may not be complicated, the substantial number of values may be difficult to understand in their “raw” form (Li, 2020: 19).

“Trees” allow data to be displayed to depict importance, structure, time stamps, evolution/growth, or any related instance (Li, 2020: 24). For example, in Figure 1, there is a clear “hierarchical” (Li, 2020: 24) flow to the “skill tree” (FIFA 23, 2022). The locked icon of the tree that is marked A cannot be unlocked unless a series of other branches is unlocked first. The structure is evident and allows the user, in this instance, the player, to view their progression accordingly. This form of data visualisation is not a conventional example like a food chain or a family tree, but it shows progression and importance, which is a fundamental concept of this data visualisation form.

“Maps” are scaled illustrations of a particular location (in a geographical context) or a representation of an aspect, concept, program, or object (Li, 2020: 26). This is a concept that should be easy to understand theoretically since we are exposed to so many, but still, I struggled to grasp it due to the idea that anything can be mapped out. For example, “DNA mapping” and Google Maps are fundamentally different however, both can be classified as a form of a map (Li, 2020: 26). The difference is that a DNA map uses a microscopic scale (this increases the magnification) whereas a map of Johannesburg needs to be scaled down to cover the entire space.

“Tables” allow for information of distinct types to be displayed in one space (Li, 2020: 30). I do think that there is a common misconception that using tables as a data visualisation form refers to an excessive number of values that are all placed within rows and columns. Tables can display text, numbers, check boxes, images, and any other form of data that needs to be compared between various categories. For example, in Figure 2, we see the classes (all the text under headings, which one may interpret as the data types that are being compared) and their corresponding numerical values, whereas, in Figure 3, the numerical values are used to distinguish the data types that are being compared and text is the data that is being displayed.

“Graphs” allow multiple numerical values of a specific or multiple category/s to be displayed visually using lines, bars, and so on (Li, 2020: 34). To some extent, I do think that graphs can be altered or “inaccurate” as the individual/s generating the graph can alter the scale or axis to suit the strategy that they are trying to achieve (Li, 2020: 35); even though the values may be correct, the representation of these values may create a false sense of understanding by the viewers. It is important to represent information honestly and ethically.

There are attributes inherent in “traditional data visualisations” (Li, 2020: 41). The focus of the data visualisation should be on what is being displayed and not on how it is being said. The viewer should easily differentiate between different values, headings, colours, and so on. Yes, graphs, scatter plots, and even maps can be challenging to comprehend, however, once the viewer develops a firm grasp of the data visualisation form, they should easily read the data. The data visualisation should rely on recall and relevancy. It should also act as an aid to understanding the data sets.

Conclusion

During my first attempt at understanding what data visualisation is, I found myself confusing the use of art to represent or explain a concept with data visualisation. After completing this reading, I do think I am in a better position to move forward with my website. Blog Post #02 - Critical Reflection - My Proposed UI and UX

My overall objective is to create a website that effectively allows the user to navigate through it and convey the theme that was set out for us. More importantly, I am hoping that with the first iteration of this website, I have improved and learned from my previous mistakes.

Colour Palette/Choice:

I have opted for a black-and-white colour palette with random bursts of colour. I believe that these random bursts of colour will draw the user to important information and guide them along with my future data visualisation additions. At the time of writing, there are no data visualisations yet, and my entire website is in black and white.

Homepage

I tend to put a lot of thought, sometimes too much thought, into my homepage and I struggle to find the balance between too little information and an unnecessary amount of information. I have opted for a very basic homepage and have found the perfect image for what I was trying to accomplish.

On my website, there is an image of an astronaut looking up giving the impression of the astronaut staring at the heading and navigation bar. At first, I thought this was a really neat idea but felt it was just too little information for the user. The astronaut guides the user to where I want them to look first however, the headings provide too little information about my website.

For example, some of the pages on my website are about Mars, The Kepler Field, and The Picture of the Day. The headings give an overall idea of what will be on the webpage however, it does not specify considering the various content that could possibly be about Mars or The Kepler Field. I have decided to implement a tooltip that appears when the user hovers over one of the buttons (or headings) and gives them more information about the page that follows. This tooltip disappears when the user's mouse leaves the button. This allows me to ensure the clean and uncluttered homepage that I desired but still allows the user to gain more information on the website.

Navigation Bar:

This is consistent throughout my website. I believe that the homepage acts as a tutorial of some sort. Keeping this idea in mind, I hope to allow my users to identify how my navigation bar works when first interacting with it and from that point onwards their navigation experience will be easier.

I do however think that the transition speeds when hovering and when the tooltip appears are too quick. It feels more like a flash rather than a transition. I noticed this before submission however I could not bring my mind to deciding the correct value of time. My idea moving forward would be to sit with a few friends and alter the time slightly each time and get some feedback; thereafter, I will decide on what works best.

Blog Page:

My blog section is split into two parts. The quick access panel acts as a table of contents. For some context, my data visualization idea would be an illustration of the Earth rotating around the sun. My concern is that this constant motion would become annoying for the user. Hence, the table of contents is labelled as a quick access panel as I would like to allow the user to have a choice: 1) Interact with my data visualization idea to navigate to the desired blog post or 2) click on the blog post heading in the quick access panel. Also, I have tested the visual illustration with no interactivity and I would like to make sure that there is a backup navigation if there are any unforeseen errors.

I have also decided to add all my blog posts on one page. The goal is to have everything under one section in one place. For navigating and user experience purposes, I do think that scrolling to the top of the page to access information would be easier than going back to a previous tab. Currently, on my website, there is a flaw with this idea: when I add this blog post, the user will need to scroll far up to access the access bar or the navigation bar. I would like to either add a scroll to the top button on the page and/or have the access bar as a slide-out menu to improve the user experience.

Design Section/Blog Post:

These two sections follow a very simple idea. Heading > Divider line > Paragraph. I kept this constant throughout both sections to ensure that the user knew how to read everything from experience. The idea is to have the user see the heading in a larger font, gain some sort of idea as to what they are going to read next, and then read the text below. I personally do not like blog pages that are in a newspaper/magazine format (with columns, an enlarged first letter of the paragraph, etc.), I just feel like a more linear structure is a more effective way to communicate thoughts. Once the user reads certain information, they can move on from it as it is not slightly left of a column divider for example. The planet-themed divider line was added to keep up with the theme of the website.

What my website lacks:

A footer. I have added this to my code but not implemented it into the website. I do think that this allows users who are at the end of a page to navigate back to a different page or back to the top of the page.

This brings me to my next point: I would like to add a button that is set to active when my user reaches the bottom of the page to return to the top of the page.

I do think that the small additions that make the user's life easier are what make a website more likely to succeed. It shows the user that the developer cares about their experience and would like to make it as smooth as possible.
